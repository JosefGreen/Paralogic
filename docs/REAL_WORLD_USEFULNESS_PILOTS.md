# Real-World Usefulness Pilots

## Purpose

These synthetic pilots test whether Paralogic improves claim-boundary
discipline beyond ordinary prose. They are realistic audit scenarios, not
empirical validation.

## Scoring

0 = no added value over ordinary prose. 5 = clearly improves the analysis and
gives an actionable safer conclusion.

| pilot | usefulness | repeatability | formalism necessity |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Policy/process fairness audit | 4 | 4 | 3 |
| Repair/accountability audit | 4 | 4 | 3 |
| AI/metric evaluation audit | 5 | 5 | 4 |

Average usefulness score: 4.33. No pilot is below 3.

## Pilot 1: Policy Or Institutional Claim Audit

Plain-language case description: A school or workplace policy applies the same
appeal deadline to everyone.

Original claim: "The policy is fair because it applies equally to everyone."

Evidence offered: The written rule has identical wording for all participants.

Implied conclusion: The outcome is fair and institutionally legitimate.

Missing warrant: Equal formal wording must be shown to provide substantively
equal access under the affected participants' actual constraints.

Claim type classification: procedural, normative, formal, empirical.

Paralogic/ISFT mechanism involved: M3 formal access substitution; M1 evidence
overclaim if the policy text is treated as sufficient evidence of fairness.

Formal-workbench connection: M3 blocks the inference from formal access to
substantive access when usability, comprehension, remedy, or boundary
conditions are missing.

What ordinary critical thinking would say: Equal wording is not always equal
access.

What Paralogic adds beyond ordinary critique: A repeatable checklist of the
missing warrant and the safer conclusion type.

Safer conclusion: The policy is formally uniform. Fairness remains undetermined
until substantive access and affected-party constraints are checked.

Required evidence for stronger conclusion: Accessibility evidence, notice
quality, accommodation process, appeal usability, affected-party feedback, and
review path.

Usefulness score: 4.

Repeatability score: 4.

Formalism necessity score: 3.

## Pilot 2: Repair Or Accountability Audit

Plain-language case description: An organization issues an apology and opens an
internal investigation after a failure.

Original claim: "The organization apologized and launched an investigation,
therefore accountability occurred."

Evidence offered: Public apology, investigation announcement, and a named
committee.

Implied conclusion: Accountability and repair have occurred.

Missing warrant: Symbolic acknowledgement and process initiation must connect
to responsibility, remedy, verification, recurrence prevention, and affected
party standing.

Claim type classification: repair, symbolic, procedural, normative.

Paralogic/ISFT mechanism involved: M5 repair failure, M4 symbolic
substitution, M9 veto suppression if affected participants cannot challenge the
process.

Formal-workbench connection: M5 separates repair need, responsible agent, plan,
action, verification, recurrence, and closure.

What ordinary critical thinking would say: An apology is not the same as repair.

What Paralogic adds beyond ordinary critique: It forces the audit to identify
which repair component is absent and prevents a closure claim before
verification.

Safer conclusion: The organization has acknowledged the issue and started a
process. Accountability and repair remain open.

Required evidence for stronger conclusion: Completed investigation, public
findings, responsible decision-maker, remedy implementation, verification,
recurrence monitoring, and affected-party review.

Usefulness score: 4.

Repeatability score: 4.

Formalism necessity score: 3.

## Pilot 3: AI Or Metric Evaluation Audit

Plain-language case description: A model improves on a benchmark dashboard.

Original claim: "The model passed the benchmark, therefore it is reliable."

Evidence offered: Benchmark score, dashboard trend, and release note.

Implied conclusion: The system is reliable in deployment.

Missing warrant: Benchmark performance must be linked to target use, construct
validity, reliability testing, external replication, and deployment context.

Claim type classification: metric, evaluator, empirical, procedural.

Paralogic/ISFT mechanism involved: M2 metric-as-value collapse; evaluator
acceptance non-collapse; empirical validation protocol boundary.

Formal-workbench connection: Countermodels block evaluator acceptance or
benchmark success from implying empirical truth; M2 blocks proxy-target
collapse.

What ordinary critical thinking would say: Benchmarks can be proxies.

What Paralogic adds beyond ordinary critique: It gives a repeatable
classification of proxy use, target divergence, evaluator boundary, and the
evidence required for a stronger deployment claim.

Safer conclusion: The model passed the benchmark. Reliability for the intended
deployment remains unproven.

Required evidence for stronger conclusion: Construct validity, target-domain
tests, reliability statistics, failure analysis, replication, monitoring, and
deployment-specific risk review.

Usefulness score: 5.

Repeatability score: 5.

Formalism necessity score: 4.

## Usefulness Check

The pilots show that the workbench can improve claim-boundary discipline in
three synthetic scenarios. The added value is strongest where a public claim
collapses a proxy, process, or symbol into a stronger conclusion.

## Claim Boundary

These pilots do not prove general real-world utility, empirical validity,
legal liability, moral guilt, harm, or institutional illegitimacy.
